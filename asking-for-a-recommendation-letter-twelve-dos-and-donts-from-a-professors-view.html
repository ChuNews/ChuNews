Asking for a Recommendation Letter: Twelve Do’s and Don’ts From a Professor’s View

Mark Chu July 20, 2026

Some are “common sense,” but there are other critical components you might not know

[Chu News — Mark Chu / Education] I think it’s fair to say that in this modern society, the majority of people have to ask for a recommendation letter at some point in their lives. After all, the majority of higher education institutions and jobs require it; in many cases more than one. Don’t think this is just a formality: recommendation letters can be critical for a job, college, or graduate program applications. However, since I started my teaching career, I’ve been surprised by the fact that many people have no clue how to ask for one. Quite a few students thought they could ask me just because of some random reasons; some didn’t even bother to ask.

One thing I have to emphasize is that the preparation for asking for a letter starts WAY EARLIER than you think. If you’re a student, this process begins when you first step on the campus soil. If you never think about it until you need to apply for a job or graduate school, chances are it’s too late. Building enough credit for a good letter is a long process. But if you’re in that position, there might still be something you can do.

I write this with the assumption that you’re either a high school or college student. However, if you’re not, I believe the same principles apply.

So first, these are things to keep in mind when you start taking classes.

1. Your grade in the class

At least in my case, even getting an A will NOT automatically earn you a letter; there’re many other factors I have to consider. If you get a C or lower in one particular class, you probably don’t want to ask that teacher.

2. Interaction

The truth is, once again: getting a good letter requires preparation—long-term.

Let’s say you just start taking my class(es).  You need to help me know you, so the least you can do—when you’re in my class(es), NOT after—is being responsive. Better yet, take initiative and ask questions. I’m not asking you to be a teacher’s pet, but you need to show enough enthusiasm or curiosity to let me notice you. Keep in mind: even if you got an A, if you think back and realize you never answered any question/message I ask, chances are I will not know you well enough to write a good letter. You’re NOT the only A student. Especially when the class is big, or it’s an online class, you should always assume the instructor doesn’t know you unless you make an effort.

Also, do your best NOT to annoy or disappoint ANY teacher. Do not think “oh, don’t worry, I won’t ask this person as my reference.” From my experience, teachers/professors talk about students among themselves, sometimes even across different majors. Once our program was discussing which student should receive a certain award, one faculty member mentioned a name, and another shook his head, “She didn’t behave well in my class.” Other faculty didn’t have a strong opinion. Lo and behold, that award was given to someone else.

On the other hand, however, we all know one or two “psycho” teachers. If you have that, no need to be paranoid. If you show your effort and sincerity in classes, people will see that and respect you, even a psycho professor can’t deny it (try to avoid her/him still). Usually, if a teacher is psycho, other teachers know that too. I indeed saw a case of a faculty member trying to pin down a student, but other faculty disagreed and prevented further harm. If you’re in a tough position, find a teacher you can trust to help you.

3. Your effort and willingness to learn

Again, getting an A doesn’t necessarily show teachers you’re willing to put the effort into the class. Some just do the minimum required in assignments. If you don’t show enough effort/motivation, the best you’ll get is a lukewarm letter.

Student A was getting good grades in one of my classes, but toward the middle of the semester he had some different ideas about his research project. I explained to him why he shouldn’t go for these ideas, but he had the guts to use another professor’s words against mine (a new faculty member who didn’t have the related experience). I constantly clarified they were not effective, but he wouldn’t listen. It didn’t affect his grade, he still got an A. Then he decided that the new faculty was a better researcher and chose to work with her on his extended project. Sure, no problem. Unfortunately, time proved I was right and his project fell apart, along with his trust in that faculty. A while later he came to me for a letter, but how could I recommend someone who wouldn’t listen to my teaching? Not to mention he was applying to the program I went to, which taught me everything I knew? See, I do NOT mind students disagreeing with me—as long as they can offer better, valid evidence, I’ll be more than happy to receive it. But not the “I just feel like you’re wrong and I want to do it my way despite your evidence” type of disagreement.

Now, you need a letter and you’re planning to ask someone…

4. Make sure you choose the right person

This might sound like common sense, but you’ll be surprised how many people don’t think this through before they ask.

Do NOT pick a person as your reference just because s/he is a teacher/professor/boss, is nice, or is a “friend” of yours. Instead, pick the reference you have taken at least one class from or have worked with for at least 4 – 6 months, AND is impressed by your work. If you have worked with a professor on a one-on-one basis (e.g., teaching assistant, research project, club officer), that will be the best reference you can get assuming you have done a good job to impress the professor. Hint: apply for a teaching or research assistant position, even if your future goal doesn’t necessarily involve teaching/research; or a club officer, and show the professor your enthusiasm and sense of responsibility.

I had a few students who didn’t do well in my class and seemed not to care. A year later when they were applying for graduate programs, they asked for a letter. I told them that their grades didn’t look good, and though undoubtedly they were smart, I didn’t see the effort. In fact, I was a bit surprised they would ask me, I guess either they chose the wrong person, or they realized they had limited choices. Sorry, too late.

Ask yourself: how was my performance in this reference’s class(es)/job? Was it impressive enough? What will this reference say about me?

5. ASK FIRST—politely and nicely

At the very least, ask the reference, “Dear Ms/Mr./Dr./Professor XX, I’m applying ____ (details, not just a link). Can you write a rec letter for me? I’ll appreciate it very much.” beforehand. That is, do NOT give the school or job the reference’s name BEFORE you ask her/him. Quite a few times I got an email out of nowhere saying “Ms./Mr. so-and-so lists you as a reference, please evaluate this applicant’s person/writing skills…” Big mistake. First, the reference probably has taught many students; no matter how famous you are, s/he might not remember/recognize you. Second, s/he might not have time. Third, you didn’t give them a choice, and nobody likes to be forced to do something, so the letter won’t be too flattering—and trust me, you do NOT want that at all. Finally, s/he might not like you or might have nothing nice to say about you in the beginning.

6. Waive the right

I don’t know about job applications, but for higher education, especially graduate schools, they usually will ask the applicant whether s/he wants to waive the right to read the recommendation letter. I strongly recommend that you choose the “waive the right” option. If you choose not to waive the right, they’ll smell something fishy, and thus won’t take the letter seriously. Yes, you can choose not to waive the right, but then the admission committee will conclude: “The evaluation may be less candid because the writer knew that the student would see it. As a result, less weight may be assigned to such letters.” Believe me, that IS BAD.

And it’s in your best interest NOT to ask any of your references to read the letter. If you’re that worried, you probably shouldn’t have asked for this reference in the first place. In my case, I’m always honest; if I have some reservations about this student, I’ll tell her/him: “You have so and so strengths, but I’m also not so sure about X and Y… if you really want me to write one, I’ll be honest and write everything. Do you still want me to write one for you?” If your potential reference is open to it, discuss with her/him your strengths and weaknesses, then you can decide if this is a good reference for you.

7. Give the reference enough time

Trust me, you don't want the reference to rush through your recommendation. You should give your reference the specific due date, and it better be at least two weeks away. A recommendation letter isn’t like writing a note to your roommate. If you wait till the last moment, you won’t like the thing s/he writes about you.

Of course, if it’s an emergency, the reference should understand. But you’ll have to explain the situation and respect her/his decision whether or not to write a letter for you.

8. Help your reference to know your strengths

No need to be lengthy (a few short paragraphs will do), but you must give the reference an introduction of your background, e.g., your strengths, high GPA, unique work experience, leadership or service experience, etc. Give him/her your resume if proper. Also, you should refresh the reference’s memory by reminding her/him what class(es) you’ve taken from her/him (including the title(s) and when), some of the good work you’ve done in her/his class(es)/the job, even what assignment(s) or project(s) you enjoyed/learned the most. By doing so, you help the reference to understand you better, how the class(es)/job has an impact on you and feel happy (which is important if you want the reference to write good things about you). Don’t lie though—it’ll kill you. The key: show your reference that you’re a seriously persistent student/worker and are reliable.

9. Give the reference the program/job brief overview

What is the program/job about? What are the requirements? What’s the address/email? When is it due? Who should the letter be addressed to? If the reference has to find details like these by themselves, you’re annoying her/him, and that’s really not good for you. I’ll suggest you explain the program/job in your own words (again, very briefly, don’t write a long essay), do not simply give the reference a website link like saying “see for yourself.”

Nowadays, most, if not all, programs/jobs receive letters through email or an online site instead of snail mail. But in case the program/job you’re applying for does, don't ask your reference to address it and put postage on it for you. If the letter has to be mailed (instead of email), the least you can do is tell the reference that you’ll be glad to provide an envelope with postage. Even if they don’t need it, they’ll appreciate your consideration. The key: you want to be the LEAST amount of trouble, so the reference is not annoyed by having to do work you could have done for them (and should have).

10. But don’t be shy if you need more than one letter

I can’t emphasize this enough: our job as a teacher is to HELP YOU SUCCEED—as long as you’ve done your part. Usually, a student will apply to more than one program or job, and we totally understand that. When I looked for a college professor job, I applied to sixty schools. Some might be afraid to bother a reference with so many letters. Don’t be. In the majority of cases, all they need to do is copy and paste.

11. Follow up

When the due date for the program or job is approaching (like several days away), you should first ask the program or job whether they’ve received the letter. If they say no, you must follow up with the reference. Ask nicely. For example: “Dear Ms./Mr./Dr./Professor XX, I appreciate your time for writing the letter for me. I checked with the program/job, and they said they haven’t received your letter. Just wondering if you have mailed it. Thanks for your help.” The reference is obligated to reply promptly and act quickly if s/he hasn’t sent the letter.

In very rare cases (but I’ve met at least one), some shitty references will NEVER send the letter and even have the guts to LIE to you that s/he has sent it. If you want to prevent this from causing irreversible damage, maybe at least prepare another name in case of emergency. When it’s obvious this fuck-up reference decides to be a jerk, contact the name right away.

12. A thank-you note will be nice

After all the things are done, the letter is sent and the program/job has given you the decision, it will be nice if you send the reference a quick thank-you note with the update. We would like to keep records, and we care about you. Plus, you might need this person’s reference again in the future.

Takeaway

A recommendation letter can be the factor that makes a difference. Starting from now, show your potential reference(s) your effort and reliability; at least to me they are what matter the most. Build good, professional relationships. They will be good resources in the future.
