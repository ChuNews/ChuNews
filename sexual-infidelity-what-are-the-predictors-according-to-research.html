Sexual Infidelity: What Are the Predictors, According to Research?

Mark Chu July 20, 2026

What kinds of people/situations are more likely to lead to affairs?

When I was a young teen, I already had a clear idea of what I’d do if my future spouse cheated on me: divorce. No other alternative. However, as I grew older, had my own family, have seen more, I gradually realized the issue was way more complicated than I’d believed.

There are, of course, tons of studies on this topic. What I’m going to do here is just review some quick summaries, and I’ll only focus on the most “stereotypical” type: sexual infidelity.

But first, how prevalent is it? It’s not that easy to find out the exact number because few people will be willing to openly admit it. In general, research suggests that it’s around 20 – 25% in America (for example, see here and here); in other words, one in about four to five people will have sex with someone other than their spouse/partner at least once. Dr. Mark at Indiana University and her colleagues stated that infidelity is the most common reason for divorce in America, and Dr. Betzig at the University of Michigan also claimed that it’s the #1 reason in at least 160 other countries. Guess too many people think with that thing between their legs and their spouses aren’t happy about it.

Dr. Atkins, a clinical psychologist at the University of Washington, and his colleagues analyzed a big data set from national surveys of around 4,000 people. Let’s break down the findings into different categories.

Demographic factors

Men vs. Women  

Yes, according to this study, men are more likely to have sexual affairs than women. But what’s more interesting is the probability changes throughout different ages: men between the ages of 55 and 65 at the time of the survey are the most likely to report infidelity (about 15%). Is it because men who reach these ages typically have the itch? Or because of the “cohort effect,” which means these male respondents grew up in a certain culture that somehow “encourages” infidelity? We don’t really know, but the researchers infer it’s because of the cohort effect.

Women have a much lower probability (around 4 – 5%), and they’re more likely to report infidelity between the ages of 40 and 45. Again, this might NOT be saying women of these ages want to have affairs; it might be the effect of the culture experienced in this particular age group, we don’t really know.

However, this study as well as Dr. Fincham at Florida State University and his colleague also pointed out that this gap between men and women is closing. Women nowadays represent a significant part of the workforce, which means they have more financial power, independence, and opportunity for infidelity.  

Education

Respondents with graduate degrees AND who have previously divorced are 1.75 times more likely to have affairs (again) than those with less than high school diplomas. The study didn’t say much about possible reasons, my guess is that these people are more likely to have status and opportunity.

Religion

People who report attending church regularly AND are highly satisfied with their marriages (reporting “very happy”) have a lower likelihood of infidelity. For those who attend church regularly but report marriage satisfaction as “pretty happy” or “not too happy,” their religious behavior isn’t associated with any lower probability of infidelity.

Looking at what other studies find, we know that religion is a complicated factor. The study in 2007 must be loved by many faithful church-goers: they found that people who see the Bible as the literal word of God are significantly less likely to have affairs. On the other hand, however, this study by Iranian researchers in 2019 and Dr. Mark’s research found no such relationship.

I think maybe one reason for the big discrepancy is that people attend church for a variety of motives. Some truly internalize the values of religious teaching, while others go to the chapel just because that’s another place to talk and maybe eat, but the surveys might have a harder time catching that. Just my speculation though.

History of divorce

Yup, people who’ve divorced before are nearly twice as likely to have affairs again than those who’ve never divorced.

Incomes/employment status

The study put incomes and employment status together as an indicator of opportunity. For incomes, among people who make $30,000/year or more (about $45,755 in 2021, see this calculator), the more they make the more likelihood of having affairs. No such relationship among people making less than $30,000. It’s suggested that with higher incomes, people might have higher status and more opportunities for traveling or seeing new people. Also, they didn’t ask the incomes of more than $75,000/year (about $114,386 in 2021); they thought that maybe for crazy-rich people the relationship would be even stronger.  

For employment status, they have four categories: respondents and their spouses both are employed, respondents and their spouses both are unemployed, respondents are employed but their spouses aren’t, and respondents aren’t employed but their spouses are. The “respondents are employed but their spouses aren’t” category is the most likely to have affairs—probably because this signifies the most power difference. The “both are unemployed” is the least likely to have affairs, guess they don’t have the time, energy, or chance.

Age of first marriage

The younger the people got married the first time, the more likely they’ll have affairs. Those who were first married at 16 (goodness) are almost 4 times more likely to have affairs than people at 23. This is expected: if you ask me, teens are, in general, impulsive, immature, and not experienced. When I was a teen I was stupid like hell, can’t imagine what would have happened if I were married at 16.

Relationship

Relationship satisfaction

People might think only those who are in unhappy marriages will go out and seek alternatives, but the researchers of this study reviewed the literature and found that some studies indicated no such relationship. Some, however, did suggest that inadequate marital qualities, e.g., low relationship satisfaction and low frequency of sex were quite significant predictors of infidelity. One of the purposes of this study was to find out which side is supported in the population they studied. And they found it: the data analyses indicated that compared to those who report “very happy” in their relationship, people who report “pretty happy” are almost 2 times more likely to have affairs, and people who report “not too happy” are 4 times more likely. Further, researchers at Pennsylvania State University suggested that low marital satisfaction can both be the cause and the consequence of infidelity.  

Note: this is by no means an excuse for affairs (“I have an affair because I’m not happy in my relationship!”); for one thing, this unhappy relationship might be your fault. It’s rarely only one partner’s fault anyway.

Commitment

In terms of relationship satisfaction, Dr. Drigotas, a psychologist at Johns Hopkins University, along with his colleagues stated that commitment is one crucial key to higher satisfaction, which predicts a lower probability of infidelity. People with a stronger commitment have higher attachment and desire to continue the relationships.

Further, people who cohabit before marriage are more likely to have affairs (for example, see this study). I think “commitment” might explain why: the idea of cohabitation is “let’s try and see if this works,” which implies a lack of commitment.  

Therefore, it seems that for a relationship to work well, both have to be committed.

Besides these factors above reviewed by Dr. Atkins and his colleagues, Dr. Fincham and his colleagues also summarized some other factors. 

Similarities

According to research, “opposites attract” doesn’t work in long-term relationships. Interestingly, according to Drs. Brooks and Monaco, the economists at the University of Wisconsin at LaCrosse, couples who are devoted to the same religion and have higher levels of education have lower probabilities of infidelity. In general, these two factors are people’s core values, and it’s hard to stay with someone with different core values.

Personality/individual

Neuroticism

People who are neurotic (emotionally unstable, prone to negative emotions such as anger, anxiety, depression; for example, see this study) are more likely to have affairs. As you can see, neurotic individuals are not happy people.  

Attachment styles

An insecure attachment style is associated with higher chances of infidelity, but this study doesn’t specify which style. Luckily, psychologists at Florida State University found that those higher in anxiety attachment are more likely to have affairs.  

Attachment styles are too huge a topic to explain here, I’ll go into details in another article. Basically, anxious style individuals are always highly on guard for the relationship, constantly worry that they’re not good enough and therefore will be abandoned; they’re those who are clingy to their partners. Strange and counter-intuitive, right? If you’re afraid to be abandoned, why would you go find someone else? We’ll talk about it in another piece.

Behavioral problems

Drug/alcohol abuse is positively related to infidelity; the more use of drugs, the higher chance of infidelity.

Attitude toward sex

Yup, of course: those who can “separate sex from love,” those who see casual sex as no big deal are more likely to f*k someone else.

Family of origin

An individual’s parent(s) have an impact too. Those who see parental infidelity are twice more likely to do it themselves than those who don’t have parental infidelity. It can be “monkey see, monkey do,” but the reasons might be way more complicated than that.

Internet

Oh yeah, the internet is another whole new world, isn’t it? This study said that 20 – 33% of people use the internet for sex, and among these people, 65% do it. To make it worse, more than half of them engage in unprotected sex. This has become a problem of spreading STDs and HIV, and therefore a costly issue for public health.

POTENTIAL APPLICATION

Okay, at this point, some of you might be getting nervous: oh no, I/my partner got many of these factors, are we doomed?

Dr. Atkins advised not to panic because of the nature of the research: this study, along with all related investigations, is correlational. In other words, they only examine relationships, not cause-and-effect. If we can gather a bunch of married people in a huge lab, randomly assign them to the “opportunity” group with many other potential sexual partners, and the “no opportunity” group with no potential sexual partners (maybe very old people?), then see what will happen in maybe three years, then we might establish cause-and-effect on whether the opportunity factor is indeed a cause. That sounds like fun, but we can’t do that.

Correlation is not causation, so we have to be careful when we interpret these findings.

However, they still offered some potential suggestions. First, because the combination of one working and one staying home is the most vulnerable type to infidelity,

…couples with one working partner might give particular attention to how to maintain a sense of equality in power and control in the marriage and develop strategies to interact with other individuals and couples together.

They also recommended couples keep working on their relationships to achieve high standards of “very happy” and high satisfaction.   

And finally, these are my takeaways. From what I’ve seen, infidelity is complicated, and sometimes it’s not necessarily 100% the fault of the one who commits adultery, but the consequence is damaging. In many cases, it doesn’t only end in divorce but much farther and longer. In general, to prevent heartbreaks, commitment is the key, as Dr. Digotas’s research indicated. It can be a little like choosing a career: you don’t jump around whenever you see problems.  

And to be able to commit, a shared, common goal or goals is a vital component. I read somewhere about this: “Love is not only in couple looking at each other, but also in them looking toward the same direction” or words to that effect.

On the other hand, however, some infidelity is from much deeper and troublesome underlying problems, e.g., addiction, mental health issues, or even abuse. They have to be addressed before any reconciliation can happen, and maybe these people need a little more empathetic eye.
