The Edinburgh People I Met Cannot Be Nicer - Part 1 of 3

Mark Chu July 5, 2026

[Chu News — Mark Chu / Travel] Can we talk about the people in Edinburgh being so nice, you can't even begin to fathom what is going on?

So this May (2026), my wife and I went to the UK for two weeks.  It was the first time we went abroad to a place other than Taiwan and America.  I chose London and Edinburgh, mostly because our oldest son went there a few years ago and couldn't stop talking about them.  I somewhat thought, "Well, if this trip isn't as good, at least we have someone else to blame."

We were a bit nervous: A place we weren't familiar with, what if we got lost?  Couldn't figure out how to pay whatever fees?  Had no means to pay?  You know, all the greenie, naive tourists' typical worries.

After a ten-hour flight with Virgin Atlantic, in which the on-flight entertainment didn't work at all, and they didn't have a regular phone charger plug-in (I guess European airlines are the same?) so I couldn't use my phone, we arrived.  

And lo and behold, right after getting out of the airport looking for The Tube, we got lost.  After aimlessly walking round and round, in and out of the Tube gates, arguing where the right places were, a couple was kind enough to notice our struggle and led us to the right way.  Not only that, they asked us where we were going and told us how to get there.  So now, Londoners made a good first impression on us.

When we walked on the street, the first things that welcomed us were cars driving on the left side and weather as stable as my mood.  This minute it was sunny, the next minute it started raining.  After a long walk dragging our suitcases, we found our hotel.  

(Looking out of the windows from our hotel stairways)

I think not only in the UK, but also in other European countries, things typically equipped by default in an American hotel room aren't found here.  For example, a mini fridge, or a microwave.  Also, there are no washer and dryer in the hotel.  No free breakfast.  I have to say the customer service was not bad though.  He started a small chat, and agreed to keep our suitcases in a storage room before check-in.

A friend of ours in London described the weather in that particular day, "Going through four seasons in one day."

We were sitting in a small Mediterranean restaurant looking at the storm in awe. The boss was outgoing and friendly, and the food was great.  We loved the lentil soup.  And the best yet was NO TIP required.  In America, the service isn't any better at all, and they demand 20% more.  

The next day, while I was walking and about to turn right, I heard the bell from a bike.  Without thinking, I kept walking, until a high schooler on a bike turning left was about to hit my face.  Right away I realized my mistake: I was walking on the right.  But the high schooler said, "Sorry," and rode away.  I believe I said sorry as well.  It was my fault, not his fault anyway, yet he apologized. 

Small things.  Though up to this point, I was convinced Londoners were just very polite and nice.

Many times while we walked around, we saw dogs without leashes.  But like their owners, they were either friendly or simply minding their own business.  By golly, are London dogs trained by the culture too?  Have gone to manners school?

My wife wanted to go to a park to see birds and took photographs.  This was the main purpose of the trip.  She found St James's Park that seemed to be worth a visit, so off we went.  But we had no idea what kind of place it was and what we were walking into.

My wife heard a lot of birds singing coming from the building on our right and said, "I need to find a way in." I saw all the sharp spikes on the top of the walls and said, "I don't think you can... this looks like a very important building."

There were many people from all over the world taking pictures, and at that moment we realized we had turned into tourists admiring this attraction.  What's funny is I've lived in America for about thirty years, and the first capitol building I've visited outside of Taiwan's government was the UK's.  No, I've never been to White House yet.

I guess he was in his 60s.  It was kind of chilly, but he wore light clothes and was barefoot.  And he was a bird whisperer.  Out of nowhere, he greeted us and started talking about the birds while feeding them.  He was knowledgeable, very sociable, and enthusiastic.  My wife got a few good bird pics. 

But suddenly we heard a scream, a teenage girl crying out loud.  Everyone turned to look at what was going on.  The girl was probably on the spectrum, and her toy or something important to her was dropped into a restricted area.  Her mom was comforting her.  I think they believed that the thing was lost forever.  A guard who was far away on the other side saw what happened, ran into the area, picked the thing up, gave it to the girl, and had a quick chat with them.  Everything was fine now.

I guess we indeed experienced a piece of the UK in the park.  We enjoyed it.

The next day, we visited Chiswick House and Gardens, right next to our hotel.  It was big, with many people jogging, walking dogs, or resting and chatting.  By now, readers should know that pretty much every garden in the UK is beautiful, but here are a few photos presented as evidence.

However, the garden, though pretty, had a smell of dogs' poop lingering everywhere.  Animals have the right to enjoy the place as much as we do, so we don't complain.

The garden has a restaurant, and right next to it is the public bathroom.  The men's room was ok, but my wife said there was neither tissue paper nor soap in the women's.  Bummer.

We have a friend in London.  He and his husband couldn't be nicer.  They brought us places and treated us to all the fancy meals.  They made sure we knew what the UK was like and our trip was worthwhile.

One of the best ways to learn about a culture, of course, is food.  They brought us to a traditional English breakfast place, Ace Cafe London.  The food was great.  Unfortunately, I didn't think to take a picture until halfway done...

We didn't know London had a lot of canals.  Some were like overpasses with boats roaming on them.  We were very amazed.

This is one canal.

 

 

It was in the afternoon, we were down on the street, and we noticed a huge crowd gathering.  They were shouting and singing.  "Football fans," our friend said.  There was a big game coming, and the whole area was packed with people, and I'm sure some (or many) with alcohol in their hands.  Sometimes they became so loud, you'd think a big fight would start any time.  But they were just very enthusiastic.

That's definitely a lovely cultural phenomenon I like to see.

Whether you know about the issues or not, whether you like it or not, I believe we agree that everyone must fully support basic human rights.  Basic.  Human. Rights.

 

The following day, our friends took us to the University of Cambridge.  I always wanted to go there; three of my favorite Brits all graduated from there -- David Attenborough, Hugh Laurie, and John Oliver (by last name alphabetical order).  It must be a great school.  By the way, Happy 100th Birthday, Sir Attenborough!

I didn't know, but the U of Cambridge is in the city of Cambridge--and there is not a "gate," "fence," or "wall" for the campus.  All the colleges, departments, buildings are spread around the city.  So we simply walked around to visit as many places as possible.

 

The next day we visited this vegetarian restaurant.  I vowed never to visit the same restaurant twice so we could try different food, good or bad.  This one was ok--and surprise, it asked for a tip.  I think I could have chosen "no tip;" after all, this is the UK, but I gave a little.  Bad habit.

The last one was arguably the best.  What do you think?

And this concluded our stay in London.  It's a great city.

Next, we head toward another great one, Edinburgh.
